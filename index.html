Logical Structure & Argument

TermMeaningExample
AbductiveInference to the best explanation.The hypothesis of evolution is abductive, not deductive.
Absurd Obviously logically untenable; a contradiction reached from an assumption. See also: reductio ad absurdum. Assuming both that moral truth is wholly relative and wholly objective leads to an absurd conclusion.
ArgumentA structured set of premises paired with a conclusion that they support.The argument fails because one premise is false.
ConclusionThe claim purportedly supported by premises.Therefore, moral realism follows.
ConsistentA set of claims can all be true together.His claims about freedom are internally consistent.
ContradictoryTwo claims cannot both be true.The statements are contradictory, not merely contrary.
CounterexampleA case that falsifies a universal claim or shows an analysis fails.Gettier cases are counterexamples to JTB.
DeductiveTruth-preserving inference; conclusion guaranteed if premises are true.The proof is deductive.
EntailsLogically implies; truth of one guarantees truth of the other.p entails q if it is impossible for p to be true and q false.
ImpliesOften used informally for 'entails', but weaker outside strictly logical contexts.This implies a contradiction, though it may not entail one.
InductiveProbabilistic or generalizing inference; conclusion not guaranteed.Hume doubts induction can be justified non‑circularly.
InferenceThe act or process of deriving a conclusion from premises.His inference from desire to value is invalid.
PremiseA statement offered in support of a conclusion.Premise (2) is ambiguous.
SoundValid and has all true premises.Modus ponens yields a sound argument when its premises are true.
Thought experimentAn imagined scenario used to test concepts or principles.Frankfurt cases are thought experiments about free will.
ValidImpossible for all premises to be true and the conclusion false.The argument is valid but unsound.

Syllogistic & Inference Forms - Patterns of valid reasoning

Form Meaning Example
Constructive Dilemma A valid argument form combining two conditionals with a disjunction. P → R; Q → S; P ∨ Q; therefore R ∨ S.
Destructive Dilemma A valid argument form: if two conditionals hold and the consequents (or one of them) fail, infer that one of the antecedents must fail. P → R; Q → S; ¬R ∨ ¬S; therefore ¬P ∨ ¬Q.
Disjunctive Syllogism A valid inference form: from “P or Q” and “not-P,” infer “Q.” P ∨ Q; ¬P; therefore Q.
Hypothetical Syllogism A valid inference: if P implies Q and Q implies R, then P implies R. P → Q; Q → R; therefore P → R.
Modus Ponens A valid inference form: from “If P then Q” and “P,” infer “Q.” P → Q; P; therefore Q.
Modus Tollens A valid inference form: from “If P then Q” and “not-Q,” infer “not-P.” P → Q; ¬Q; therefore ¬P.
Reductio ad Absurdum An argument form in which one assumes a proposition and derives a contradiction, thereby showing the original assumption false. Assume P; derive a contradiction; therefore ¬P.

Modal & Metaphysical Terms

TermMeaningExample
AccidentalContingent property, not essential.Being musical is accidental to Socrates.
Categorical base The underlying non-dispositional property that grounds a disposition. On some theories, a disposition (e.g., fragility) exists because there is a categorical property (e.g., microstructural arrangement) that serves as its basis. The fragility of glass is grounded in its categorical base, namely its molecular structure.
Conceptual necessityTrue in virtue of the concept’s meaning.A triangle necessarily has three sides.
ContingentTrue but could have been otherwise.Socrates was Greek contingently.
Disposition A property defined by what its bearer is inclined to do under certain conditions. Typical examples include fragility, solubility, and elasticity. Dispositions contrast with categorical properties, which have no built-in “tendency.” Salt has the disposition to dissolve in water.
EssentialBelonging to the nature or definition of a thing.Rationality is often said to be essential to humanity.
IdentityNumerical sameness, not mere similarity.Water is identical with H₂O.
ImpossibleTrue in no possible world.A square circle is impossible.
inhere A metaphysical relation in which a property exists in a substance or bearer and depends on that substance for its existence. Properties, attributes, or tropes are often said to inhere in the objects that possess them. The redness of the apple inheres in the apple itself.
NecessaryTrue in all possible worlds; could not be otherwise.A bachelor is necessarily unmarried.
Necessary conditionMust be present for the outcome to occur.Having DNA is necessary for being human.
PossibleTrue in at least one possible world.It is possible that life exists elsewhere.
Sufficient conditionIf present, guarantees the outcome.Being a triangle is sufficient for having three sides.
SuperveneHigher‑level facts depend on lower‑level facts.Moral facts supervene on natural facts.

Epistemology & Semantics

TermMeaningExample
A posterioriKnowable only through experience.Water boils at ~100°C a posteriori.
A prioriKnowable independently of experience.2 + 2 = 4 is knowable a priori.
AnalyticTrue by meaning alone.'All bachelors are unmarried' is analytic.
Category errorAssigning something to the wrong logical type.Asking where 'meaning' is located is a category error.
Conceptual analysisClarifying meaning by examining use and logical relations.We begin with a conceptual analysis of 'knowledge'.
CounterfactualA claim about what would be true if the world were different, evaluated via possible‑worlds semantics.If Kant had lived today, his political philosophy would have looked different.
Deflationary (of truth)Treats 'truth' as a logical device, not a substantive property.A deflationary theory explains truth as disquotation.
EmpiricalDerived from observation or experiment.The claim is empirical, not conceptual.
Empirical adequacyAgreement with observation.The theory is empirically adequate.
Possible worldsHypothetical total ways things could be.Kripke uses possible‑worlds semantics.
PropositionThe content of a statement that can be true or false.Beliefs have propositional content.
SyntheticTruth depends on how the world is.'The cat is on the mat' is synthetic.
Truth‑conditionsCircumstances under which a proposition is true.For 'Snow is white' to be true, snow must be white.

Philosophy of Mind & Consciousness

Term Meaning Example
qualia The subjective, qualitative aspects of conscious experience—what it is like to feel pain, see red, taste chocolate, or hear a violin. Central to debates about consciousness, physicalism, and the mind–body problem. Mary learns what seeing red is like only when she experiences the quale of red.

Normativity & Reasons

TermMeaningExample
AutonomySelf‑legislation; acting according to reasons one gives oneself.For Kant, autonomy constitutes freedom.
ConsequentialismEvaluates actions by outcomes.Utilitarianism is consequentialist.
DeontologicalDuty‑based ethical theory.Kant's ethics is deontological.
DescriptiveStates how things are.The report is descriptive, not normative.
HeteronomyBeing governed by external causes or desires.Desire makes the will heteronomous.
NormativeConcerned with what ought to be.Ethics is a normative discipline.
Reason‑givingProviding considerations that count in favor of an action.The moral law must be reason‑giving.
SupererogatoryMorally good but not required.Donating half your income is supererogatory.
TeleologicalGoal‑oriented explanation.Aristotle’s biology is teleological.

Latin & Classical Phrases

TermMeaningExample
a fortioriFrom stronger reason.If lying is wrong, perjury is a fortiori wrong.
a posteriori / a prioriAfter / before experience.Mathematics is mostly a priori.
ad hocFor this purpose only; without independent support.The ad hoc fix rescues the theory.
ceteris paribusAll else being equal.Many social‑science laws are ceteris paribus.
de facto / de jureIn fact / by law.De jure equality may not ensure de facto equality.
de se, de re, de dicto Three related distinctions used in philosophy of language, mind, and modality.

de dicto: concerning the proposition as a whole; the modal or attitude operator has wide scope over the entire statement.
de re: concerning the thing; the modal or attitude operator applies to an object, not to the whole proposition.
de se: concerning oneself; a self-locating or first-personal attitude that cannot be reduced to the de dicto form. 		de dicto: “Necessarily, the number of planets is 8.”
de re: “The number 8 is necessarily the number of planets.”
de se: “I believe that I (myself) am in danger.”
ex hypothesiBy hypothesis.Ex hypothesi, the agent is omniscient.
ipso factoBy that very fact.He violated the rule and ipso facto lost immunity.
mutatis mutandisWith necessary changes having been made.The same applies, mutatis mutandis.
per seIn itself.Knowledge per se has value.
post hoc, ergo propter hocA fallacy: after this, therefore because of this.Beware the post hoc fallacy.
prima facieAt first appearance.We have a prima facie duty not to lie.
reductio ad absurdumDerive a contradiction from an assumption.Assume p; derive absurdity; hence not‑p.
sub specie aeternitatisUnder the aspect of eternity.Spinoza urges us to see things sub specie aeternitatis.
sui generisOf its own kind.Consciousness may be sui generis.

Assumptive & Foundational Terms

TermMeaningExample
AssumptionProposition taken as true within an argument for exploration.Assume determinism; then moral responsibility becomes problematic.
AxiomFoundational truth taken as given.Nothing can be both true and false.
Grant / ConcedeAccept temporarily for dialectical purposes.Grant that desires cause actions….
HypothesisProposed assumptiontested for explanation.The hypothesis is that all knowledge derives from experience.
PostulateA necessary assumption for a system.Freedom is a postulate of practical reason.
PremiseExplicit starting point of an argument.Premise (1): All humans are mortal.
PresumeTreat as true with prior probability.We presume testimony is generally reliable.
PresuppositionBackground assumption required for meaningfulness.‘John stopped smoking’ presupposes that he once smoked.
StipulationDefinition introduced by fiat.Let us stipulate that 'agent' means a being capable of action.
SuppositionHypothetical assumption.Suppose there were a brain in a vat.
