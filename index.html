Common Analytical Philosophy Terms

A Reference Guide for Key Concepts

A Priori / A Posteriori

A Priori: Knowledge that is independent of experience. For example, mathematical truths like "2+2=4" are known a priori.

A Posteriori: Knowledge that depends on empirical evidence or experience. For example, "The sky is blue" is known a posteriori.

Etymology: From Latin, meaning "from the earlier" and "from the later"

Analytic / Synthetic

Analytic: A statement whose truth is determined by the meanings of its terms. For example, "All bachelors are unmarried."

Synthetic: A statement whose truth depends on facts about the world. For example, "All bachelors are happy."

Key distinction introduced by Immanuel Kant and refined by logical positivists

Modal Logic

The study of necessity and possibility. Modal logic extends classical logic with operators for "necessarily" (□) and "possibly" (◇).

Used to analyze statements about what must be true, what could be true, and what is actually true.

Central to discussions of metaphysical possibility and counterfactuals

Supervenience

A relationship between two sets of properties where one set (A) supervenes on another (B) if there can be no change in A without a change in B.

Example: Mental properties are often said to supervene on physical properties—no mental change without a physical change.

Important in philosophy of mind and discussions of reduction

Truth-Maker Theory

The view that true propositions are made true by things in the world—truth-makers.

For example, the proposition "Snow is white" is made true by the whiteness of snow.

Connects truth to ontology and grounds truth in reality

Intentionality

The property of mental states whereby they are about or directed toward objects or states of affairs.

For example, beliefs, desires, and thoughts all have intentionality—they are about something.

Central concept in philosophy of mind, from Brentano to contemporary discussions

Qualia

The subjective, qualitative aspects of conscious experience. "What it's like" to experience something.

For example, the redness of red, the painfulness of pain, or the taste of chocolate.

Key issue in debates about consciousness and the hard problem of consciousness

Logical Positivism

A philosophical movement that held only statements verifiable through empirical observation or logical analysis are meaningful.

Associated with the Vienna Circle and philosophers like A.J. Ayer.

Influential in early 20th century, though now largely rejected in its original form
