Analytic Philosophy Glossary (Alphabetical by Section)

Logical Structure & Argument

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
Abductive Inference to the best explanation. The hypothesis of evolution is abductive, not deductive.
Argument A set of premises intended to support a conclusion. The argument fails because one premise is false.
Conclusion The claim purportedly supported by premises. Therefore, moral realism follows.
Consistent A set of claims can all be true together. His claims about freedom are internally consistent.
Contradictory Two claims cannot both be true. The statements are contradictory, not merely contrary.
Counterexample A case that falsifies a universal claim or shows an analysis fails. Gettier cases are counterexamples to JTB.
Deductive Truth-preserving inference; conclusion guaranteed if premises are true. The proof is deductive.
Entails Logically implies; truth of one guarantees truth of the other. p entails q if it is impossible for p to be true and q false.
Implies Often used informally for 'entails', but weaker outside strictly logical contexts. This implies (suggests) a contradiction, though it may not entail one.
Inductive Probabilistic or generalizing inference; conclusion not guaranteed. Hume doubts that induction can be justified non-circularly.
Inference The act or process of deriving a conclusion from premises. His inference from desire to value is invalid.
Premise A statement offered in support of a conclusion. Premise (2) is ambiguous.
Sound Valid and has all true premises. Modus ponens yields a sound argument when its premises are true.
Thought experiment An imagined scenario used to test concepts or principles. Frankfurt cases are thought experiments about free will.
Valid Impossible for all premises to be true and the conclusion false. The argument is valid but unsound.

Modal & Metaphysical Terms

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
Accidental Contingent property, not essential. Being musical is accidental to Socrates.
Conceptual necessity True in virtue of the concept’s meaning. A triangle necessarily has three sides.
Contingent True but could have been otherwise. Socrates was Greek contingently.
Essential Belonging to the nature or definition of a thing. Rationality is often said to be essential to humanity.
Identity Numerical sameness, not mere similarity. Water is identical with H₂O.
Impossible True in no possible world. A square circle is impossible.
Necessary True in all possible worlds; could not be otherwise. A bachelor is necessarily unmarried.
Necessary condition Must be present for the outcome to occur. Having DNA is (biologically) necessary for being human.
Possible True in at least one possible world. It's possible that life exists elsewhere.
Sufficient condition If present, guarantees the outcome. Being a triangle is sufficient for having three sides.
Supervene No change in higher-level facts without some change in base-level facts. Moral facts supervene on natural facts.

Epistemology & Semantics

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
A posteriori Knowable only through experience. Water boils at ~100°C at sea level a posteriori.
A priori Knowable independently of experience. 2 + 2 = 4 is knowable a priori.
Analytic True by meaning alone. 'All bachelors are unmarried' is analytic.
Category error Assigning something to the wrong logical type. Asking where 'meaning' is located is a category error.
Conceptual analysis Clarifying meaning by examining use and logical relations. We begin with a conceptual analysis of 'knowledge'.
Deflationary (of truth) Treats 'is true' as a logical device rather than a substantive property. A deflationary theory explains truth as disquotation.
Empirical Derived from observation or experiment. The claim is empirical, not conceptual.
Empirical adequacy Agreement with observation (often in philosophy of science). The theory is empirically adequate.
Possible worlds Hypothetical total ways things could be; used to analyze modality. Kripke uses possible worlds semantics.
Proposition The content of a statement that can be true or false. Beliefs have propositional content.
Synthetic Truth depends on how the world is. 'The cat is on the mat' is synthetic.
Truth-conditions Circumstances under which a proposition is true. For 'Snow is white' to be true, snow must be white.

Normativity & Reasons

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
Autonomy Self-legislation; acting according to reasons one gives oneself. For Kant, autonomy constitutes freedom.
Consequentialism Evaluates actions by outcomes. Utilitarianism is consequentialist.
Deontological Duty-based ethical theory. Kant's ethics is deontological.
Descriptive States how things are, without evaluating. The report is descriptive, not normative.
Heteronomy Being governed by external causes or desires. Desire makes the will heteronomous.
Normative Concerned with what ought to be (reasons, duties, values). Ethics is a normative discipline.
Reason-giving Providing considerations that count in favor of a claim or action. The moral law must be reason-giving.
Supererogatory Beyond duty; morally good but not required. Giving away half your income is supererogatory.
Teleological Goal-oriented explanation. Aristotle's biology is teleological.

Latin & Classical Phrases

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
a fortiori From stronger reason: if one case holds, a stronger one does too. If lying is wrong, perjury is a fortiori wrong.
a posteriori / a priori After / before experience. Mathematics is mostly a priori.
ad hoc For this purpose only; lacking independent support. The ad hoc fix rescues the theory but lacks justification.
ceteris paribus All else being equal; holding background conditions fixed. Laws in social science are often ceteris paribus.
de facto / de jure In fact / by law. De jure equality can coexist with de facto inequality.
ex hypothesi By hypothesis; within the assumed framework. Ex hypothesi, the agent is omniscient.
ipso facto By that very fact. He violated the rule and ipso facto lost immunity.
mutatis mutandis With necessary changes having been made. The same applies, mutatis mutandis, to perception.
per se In itself; by itself. Knowledge per se has value.
post hoc, ergo propter hoc After this, therefore because of this (a fallacy). Beware the post hoc fallacy.
prima facie At first appearance; defeasible without further evidence. We have a prima facie duty not to lie.
reductio ad absurdum Reduction to absurdity: derive a contradiction from an assumption. Assume p; derive absurdity; hence not-p.
sub specie aeternitatis Under the aspect of eternity; timeless perspective. Spinoza invites us to see things sub specie aeternitatis.
sui generis Of its own kind; unique. Consciousness may be sui generis.

Modified English Verbs & Markers (Analytic Usage)

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
analyze Decompose a concept into its constitutive conditions. We analyze 'knowledge' into belief, truth, justification.
argue Present structured reasons toward a conclusion. I argue that meaning is use.
as such In its own right or essence. Knowledge as such is valuable.
assume Take as true without proof in the present context. Assume determinism.
by hypothesis / ex hypothesi Within the assumed framework. Ex hypothesi, the agent is omniscient.
characterize Describe essential features without full analysis. He characterizes moral realism as belief in objective moral facts.
claim Assert a position as true or defensible. He claims that consciousness is non-computational.
clarify Remove ambiguity or vagueness. The author clarifies 'direct perception'.
confirm Raise the probability of a hypothesis being true. O confirms T in light of background B.
consistent with Not ruled out by; compatible. Compatibilism is consistent with determinism.
contend Assert with emphasis, often against opposition. I contend that indexicals are essential to thought.
contradict Be jointly impossible. Freedom and strict determinism contradict each other (on some views).
corroborate Provide independent evidence in favor of a claim. Later studies corroborate the interpretation.
define Specify necessary and sufficient conditions for correct application. Plato defines knowledge as justified true belief.
demonstrate Prove conclusively, usually deductively or by necessary implication. The proof demonstrates that no consistent system proves its own consistency.
deny / reject Withhold assent to a claim. He denies that qualia can be reduced.
distinguish Identify relevant conceptual or logical differences. Distinguish causal explanation from rational justification.
elucidate Make clear by explanatory remarks rather than definition. Wittgenstein aims to elucidate linguistic rules.
entail Logically imply; stronger than casual 'imply'. Being a triangle entails having three sides.
establish Make a claim defensible within the argument’s context; often evidential. This establishes the plausibility of compatibilism.
follow Be a logical consequence of. From (1)–(3) it follows that compatibilism holds.
for the sake of argument Introduces a temporary concession. For the sake of argument, accept determinism.
grant / concede Accept a premise (perhaps dialectically) for argument’s sake. Grant that desires cause actions; even so...
indicate / suggest Provide some evidence for; weaker than 'show'. The cases suggest but do not show that perception is representational.
insofar as Limits the scope of a claim. The act is rational insofar as it follows the CI.
maintain Sustain a claim across objections. They maintain that moral properties are natural.
presuppose Require as a precondition of meaningfulness or truth. Promises presuppose agency.
prove Give a deductively valid argument from accepted premises. We prove the lemma by induction.
rebut Offer counter-reasons against a claim. We rebut the skeptic by presenting closure-safe knowledge.
refute Show that an argument is invalid or a conclusion false. Kripke refutes descriptivism about names.
show Provide a reasoned argument establishing a conclusion; weaker than 'prove' but stronger than 'suggest'. The Gettier cases show that JTB is insufficient for knowledge.
specify Fix reference or narrow a claim precisely. We specify 'reason' as instrumental rather than pure.
strictly speaking Invokes a narrow, technical sense. Strictly speaking, reasons are not causes.
support Count as evidence in favor of a claim (inductive/abductive). The data support the functionalist account.
suppose Temporarily assume for the sake of reasoning. Suppose the mind is identical to the brain.
undermine Reduce justification or confidence without fully refuting. These cases undermine the transparency thesis.

Assumptive & Foundational Terms

Term / PhraseTechnical MeaningTypical Analytic Usage
Assumption A proposition temporarily taken as true within an argument to explore implications; not necessarily endorsed. Assume that determinism is true; then moral responsibility seems problematic.
Axiom Foundational truth taken as given within a formal system; often necessarily true. Nothing can be both true and false (non-contradiction).
Grant / Concede Rhetorical acceptance of a claim (often temporarily) to advance dialectic. Grant that desires cause actions; even so, reasons still explain them.
Hypothesis An assumption posited for explanatory or predictive purposes; open to testing/evaluation. The hypothesis is that all knowledge derives from experience.
Postulate A declared or axiomatic assumption treated as foundational to a system. Freedom is a postulate of practical reason (Kant).
Premise An explicit starting point in an argument from which the conclusion follows. Premise (1): All humans are mortal.
Presume Treat as true without proof but with prior probability or convention; weaker than 'assume'. We presume testimony is generally reliable.
Presupposition A background assumption required for a statement to be meaningful or true; typically implicit. ‘John stopped smoking’ presupposes that John once smoked.
Stipulation A definition introduced by fiat for the purposes of the discussion. Let us stipulate that 'agent' means any being capable of intentional action.
Supposition Imaginative or hypothetical assumption; often weaker than 'assumption'. Suppose there were a brain in a vat; how would we know?
